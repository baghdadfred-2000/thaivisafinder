Quick answers

Thailand visa questions we hear a lot

Short, honest answers to the questions that bring most people here. Use the finder above for advice tailored to your own situation.

What is the best Thailand visa for retirement?+

For most people aged 50+, the Non-O Retirement visa is the standard route — 800,000 THB in a Thai bank or 65,000 THB/month income, renewable each year. Higher-income retirees (about US$80,000+/yr in passive income) often prefer the 10-year LTR Wealthy Pensioner, which adds tax benefits and only annual reporting.

Can I work in Thailand on the DTV visa?+

The DTV lets you work remotely for employers and clients outside Thailand, but you cannot legally work for Thai companies or Thai clients on it. Local work needs a Non-B visa plus a work permit, or an LTR/SMART visa in specific cases.

How long can I stay in Thailand without a visa in 2026?+

Visa-exempt entry was 60 days, but in May 2026 the Cabinet approved cutting it back to 30 days for most nationalities, pending publication in the Royal Gazette. Plan around 30 days, confirm your nationality's current allowance with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, and you can usually extend once at immigration.

What is the cheapest long-stay visa for Thailand?+

The DTV is one of the cheapest at roughly 10,000 THB for five years (180 days per entry). If you're married to a Thai citizen, the marriage visa has a low bar — 400,000 THB in the bank or 40,000 THB/month.

How much money do I need to live in Thailand?+

A modest single-person lifestyle realistically runs about US$900–1,500+ per month in 2026, and more in Bangkok or on the islands. Most long-stay visas also require proof of funds you keep set aside — commonly US$12,000–24,000+, depending on the route.
