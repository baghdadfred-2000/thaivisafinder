The full board

The Eighteen Legal Long-Stay Routes, Explained

The heart of thaivisafinder.com — and the reason it can claim completeness where others cannot — is that it weighs all eighteen legal long-stay routes rather than the handful an agency profits from.

Short-Stay & Entry Routes

Visa Exemption

Short visits of 30 to 60 days (a figure in transition as of 2026), typically extendable once at immigration. Suits long visits and exploratory trips rather than settling.

Tourist Visa (TR)

Applied for in advance, grants 60 days per entry with an extension available. Particularly useful for travellers whose passports don't receive generous visa-free treatment.

Remote Workers & Location-Independent Professionals

Destination Thailand Visa (DTV)

A five-year, multiple-entry visa granting 180 days per entry, aimed at remote workers, freelancers, and "soft-power" pursuits like Muay Thai, Thai cooking, and meditation. Typically requires around 500,000 THB in savings. Caveat: permits remote work for employers/clients outside Thailand only, not local employment.

LTR Work-from-Thailand Professional

Targets senior remote professionals employed by established foreign companies: ten-year status, favourable tax treatment on foreign income, and light annual reporting instead of the usual 90-day cycle.

SMART Visa

For skilled experts, senior specialists, investors, and startup founders in targeted, high-priority industries — technology, science, engineering, digital, biotech, and innovation.

Retirement Routes

Non-O Retirement Visa

The standard route for most people aged 50+: 800,000 THB seasoned in a Thai bank, or 65,000 THB/month in income, renewable annually. Lower-hassle than its reputation suggests.

Non-OA Long-Stay Visa

Applied for from your home country, requires qualifying health insurance, offers up to a year of multiple-entry status, extendable in-country.

Non-O-X

A five-year retirement visa for a limited list of nationalities who can show substantial funds — a longer-horizon option that reduces renewal frequency.

LTR Wealthy Pensioner

Ten-year visa for retirees with roughly US$80,000+ in annual passive income, adding tax advantages and annual (not 90-day) reporting.

Wealth & Premium Routes

LTR Wealthy Global Citizen

For high-net-worth individuals with US$1 million+ in assets, including a qualifying investment in Thailand: ten-year status and foreign-income tax exemption.

Thailand Privilege

A one-time fee in exchange for 5–20 years of residence privileges, fast-track immigration, and concierge services, with no income or age test. Rarely first choice unless you have substantial savings and plan to actually spend time in-country.

Family Routes

Marriage Visa (Non-O)

For spouses of Thai citizens: a comparatively low bar of 400,000 THB in the bank or 40,000 THB/month, can be paired with a work permit. Trade-off: heightened scrutiny, interviews, and possible home visits.

Family / Dependent Visa (Non-O)

Covers parents of a Thai child and dependents of a work-permit or long-stay visa holder, with proof of the qualifying relationship and funds.

Local Work Routes

Non-B Visa + Work Permit

The correct — indeed the only fully legitimate — path for earning money inside Thailand. Requires a Thai employer or qualifying Thai company as sponsor.

Study, Culture & Special-Purpose Routes

Education (ED) Visa

One of the most accessible, underrated long-stay options. Open across the age range, no large financial threshold, covers Thai language courses, university degree programmes, martial-arts training, and other approved study. Does not permit working and carries attendance requirements.

Medical Visa (Non-O)

Supports long-term treatment at a Thai hospital, requiring a confirmation letter from the treating facility.

Volunteer Visa (Non-O)

For genuine work with an approved NGO or charity, requiring the organisation's sponsorship and support.

Before you land

Getting the Border Basics Right

Whatever visa you ultimately choose, a handful of practical border realities catch people out, and the entry checklist on thaivisafinder.com exists to make sure they don't catch you.

  • TDAC (Thailand Digital Arrival Card) — mandatory and free, filed in the 72 hours before you land, through the official government system only. Anyone charging you a fee for it is running a scam.
  • Passport validity — must be valid for at least six months beyond your travel dates.
  • Proof of funds — commonly around 20,000 THB per person or 40,000 THB per family; officers may want to see it in cash rather than a banking app.
  • Onward or return ticket and a booked first night, ready to show if asked.
FAQ

Straight Answers to the Questions Everyone Asks

What is the best Thailand visa for retirement?

For most people aged 50 and over, the Non-O Retirement visa is the standard route — 800,000 THB in a Thai bank or 65,000 THB per month in income, renewed annually. Higher-income retirees with roughly US$80,000+ in annual passive income often prefer the ten-year LTR Wealthy Pensioner, which adds tax benefits and only annual reporting.

Can I work in Thailand on the DTV visa?

The DTV lets you work remotely for employers and clients outside Thailand, but not for Thai companies or Thai clients. Local work requires a Non-B visa plus a work permit, or an LTR/SMART visa in specific cases.

How long can I stay in Thailand without a visa in 2026?

Visa-exempt entry was 60 days, but in May 2026 the Cabinet approved cutting it back to 30 days for most nationalities, pending publication in the Royal Gazette. Plan around 30 days, confirm your nationality's current allowance with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, and you can usually extend once at immigration.

What is the cheapest long-stay visa for Thailand?

The DTV is among the cheapest at roughly 10,000 THB for five years of validity (180 days per entry). If you're married to a Thai citizen, the marriage visa also has a low bar — 400,000 THB in the bank or 40,000 THB per month.

How much money do I need to live in Thailand?

A modest single-person lifestyle realistically runs about US$900–1,500 or more per month in 2026 (more in Bangkok or on the islands). Most long-stay visas also require proof of funds you keep set aside — commonly US$12,000–24,000 or more, depending on the route.
