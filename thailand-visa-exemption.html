Thailand Visa Exemption: Short-Term Visits (30–60 Days in Transition)

The Thailand Visa Exemption, also known as visa-free entry, allows eligible foreign nationals to enter Thailand without applying for a visa in advance.

The Thailand Visa Exemption, also known as visa-free entry, allows eligible foreign nationals to enter Thailand without applying for a visa in advance. It is the most straightforward option for short tourist visits, brief business engagements, urgent or ad-hoc work, or transit. As of July 16, 2026, nationals of 93 designated countries and territories — including the United States — can receive permission to stay for up to 60 days upon arrival by air or land. This permission can normally be extended once for an additional 30 days at a Thai Immigration Bureau office, for a maximum authorized stay of 90 days without obtaining a formal visa.

Introduction

Introduction

This 60-day scheme was introduced in July 2024 as part of Thailand’s post-pandemic tourism recovery measures. However, the policy is currently in transition. In May 2026, the Thai Cabinet approved a significant reduction that would return most of the 93 eligible countries to a 30-day exemption period, with a small number of countries potentially limited to 15 days. As of July 16, 2026, this reduction has not yet been published in the Royal Gazette and therefore has not taken effect. Travelers should monitor official announcements closely, as the change could be implemented with only 15 days’ notice once published.

The visa exemption is best suited for genuine short-term visitors. It is not designed for long-term living in Thailand. People who wish to stay longer than 90 days per entry should plan to apply for an appropriate long-stay visa (such as Non-O Retirement, DTV, ED Education, or Marriage visa) rather than relying on repeated visa exemption entries.

Nationality and Eligibility Overview

Nationality and Eligibility Overview

The current 60-day visa exemption applies to citizens of 93 countries and territories. The United States is included in this list. Other major eligible nationalities include most European Union countries, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia, and several countries in the Middle East and Latin America.

For United States citizens: US passport holders qualify for the full 60-day exemption on ordinary tourist passports. No pre-approval or embassy visit is required for stays within the permitted period. Diplomatic or official US passports may receive different treatment under separate agreements, but standard tourist passports follow the 60-day rule.

Eligibility is based on nationality and passport type. The exemption is generally not available to holders of refugee travel documents or certain special passports. Immigration officers retain full discretion to deny entry even to eligible nationalities if they have concerns about the traveler’s intentions or documentation.

Detailed Requirements

Detailed Requirements

To enter Thailand under the visa exemption, travelers must meet several straightforward but important conditions:

  • Passport validity: Your passport must have at least six months of validity remaining from the date of entry into Thailand. This is strictly enforced.
  • Onward or return ticket: You should be able to present proof of onward or return travel. Airlines and immigration officers frequently request this documentation.
  • Sufficient funds and accommodation: Although there is no fixed financial deposit requirement, officers may ask for evidence of sufficient funds and accommodation arrangements, especially if your travel plans appear unclear. Enforcement of this requirement has increased in 2026.
  • Thailand Digital Arrival Card (TDAC): All travelers, including those entering under visa exemption, must complete the TDAC online within three days before arrival. This replaces the old paper arrival card and is mandatory for air, land, and sea entries.
  • Genuine visitor intent: You must intend to leave Thailand at or before the end of your permitted stay unless you obtain an extension or change to another visa status. Repeated short-stay entries without clear purpose are increasingly scrutinized.

There is no minimum age or income requirement for the initial visa exemption entry. However, the extension is granted at the discretion of the immigration officer and is not guaranteed.

Application Process and Documents

Application Process and Documents

Initial Entry (No Pre-Approval Required)

The visa exemption requires no application in advance. Upon arrival at immigration, eligible travelers simply present their passport and supporting documents. If approved, the immigration officer will stamp the passport with the permitted stay (currently up to 60 days). No visa fee is charged for the initial entry.

Extending Your Stay

You may apply for one 30-day extension at any Thai Immigration Bureau office before your current permission expires. The process is as follows:

  • Complete Form TM.7 (available at the immigration office or downloadable online).
  • Prepare the following documents: valid passport with photocopies of the bio-data page and current entry stamp, one recent 4x6 cm passport photo, proof of address in Thailand (TM.30 receipt, rental agreement, or hotel booking), and proof of onward travel or sufficient funds if requested.
  • Pay the extension fee of 1,900 THB.
  • Submit the application and wait for the officer’s decision. Processing is usually same-day if submitted early.

The extension, if granted, allows you to remain in Thailand for an additional 30 days from the original expiry date. You cannot extend a second time under the visa exemption. If you need to stay longer, you must either leave Thailand and re-enter or apply to change your visa status to another category while in Thailand.

Current Rules and Pending Changes (as of July 16, 2026)

Current Rules and Pending Changes (as of July 16, 2026)

As of July 16, 2026, the 60-day visa exemption plus one 30-day extension remains the published rule for the 93 eligible countries, including the United States. Official sources such as the US Embassy in Thailand and Thai embassies abroad continue to list the 60-day period.

On May 19, 2026, the Thai Cabinet approved a major policy change that will reduce the visa exemption period from 60 days to 30 days for most of the 93 countries, with three island nations (Maldives, Mauritius, and Seychelles) potentially limited to 15 days. The Visa on Arrival list is also being significantly reduced. This change requires publication in the Royal Gazette and will take effect 15 days after publication. As of July 16, 2026, the Royal Gazette publication has not occurred, so the 60-day rule remains in force.

In parallel, Thai immigration has increased enforcement against frequent visa runs and travelers who appear to be using repeated short-stay entries to reside in Thailand long-term. Officers now have clearer guidance to deny entry or question travelers whose entry history suggests this pattern.

Pros and Cons

Pros and Cons

Pros

  • No advance visa application or embassy visit required.
  • Fast and simple entry for short visits (tourism, business meetings, urgent work).
  • One 30-day extension available, allowing up to 90 days total per entry.
  • Low cost — only the 1,900 THB extension fee if used.
  • Widely available to US citizens and 92 other nationalities.

Cons

  • Maximum 90 days per entry without changing to another visa status.
  • Extension is discretionary and not guaranteed.
  • Increasing scrutiny on frequent visa runs; multiple entries can result in entry denial.
  • The 60-day period is transitional and may soon be reduced to 30 days with limited advance notice.
  • Not suitable for long-term residence without transitioning to a proper long-stay visa.
Requirements Checklist

Requirements Checklist

For Initial Entry:

  • Valid passport with minimum 6 months validity remaining
  • Proof of onward or return travel (recommended)
  • Completed TDAC online within 3 days before arrival
  • Proof of sufficient funds and accommodation (if requested)
  • Genuine visitor intent

Additional Items for Extension Application:

  • Form TM.7
  • One recent 4x6 cm passport photograph
  • Photocopy of passport bio-data page and current entry stamp
  • Proof of address in Thailand (TM.30 or equivalent)
  • 1,900 THB extension fee
Practical Tips, Common Pitfalls, and Related Considerations

Practical Tips, Common Pitfalls, and Related Considerations

The visa exemption works well for short visits or as a temporary bridge while preparing a longer-stay visa. Many foreigners already living in Thailand (including in popular areas such as Pattaya, Jomtien, Bangkok, and Chiang Mai) use it while transitioning to other visa categories.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Assuming the 60-day rule is permanent: The policy is in active transition. Monitor the Royal Gazette and official embassy websites for the effective date of the reduction to 30 days.
  • Waiting until the last day to extend: Apply for the 30-day extension at least several days before your current permission expires to avoid complications.
  • Frequent visa runs without clear purpose: Immigration officers are now more likely to question or deny entry to travelers who show a pattern of repeated short stays with no clear reason.
  • Forgetting the TDAC: Complete the Thailand Digital Arrival Card online before travel. Failure to do so can cause delays at immigration.

Overstay Consequences

Overstaying the visa exemption or an approved extension incurs a fine of 500 THB per day, up to a maximum of 20,000 THB. Serious or repeated overstays can result in detention, deportation at your own expense, and blacklisting from future entry into Thailand. It is always better to leave on time or obtain proper permission to stay longer.

Transitioning to Longer Stays

If you are already in Thailand on a visa exemption and wish to stay longer, you have several realistic options. Many people apply for an ED (Education) visa as a stepping stone, enroll in a language school, and then transition to other categories. Others qualify for the DTV (Destination Thailand Visa) if they meet remote work or soft-power criteria. If you are married to a Thai citizen, the Non-O Marriage visa is often the cleanest long-term solution. Retirement visa (Non-O) options exist for those aged 50 and above who meet the financial requirements.

The visa exemption is a useful short-term tool, but it is not a substitute for proper long-stay planning. Travelers who intend to spend extended periods in Thailand should begin the process of securing an appropriate visa well before their 90-day maximum is reached.
